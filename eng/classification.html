Musical Instruments Classification (MusIC)

Home > Projects > Musical Instruments Classification (MusIC)

 

The action-research project Musical Instruments Classification (MusIC) presents a critical review of the Hornbostel & Sachs classification (originally inspired by the Dewey Decimal Classification for libraries). In parallel, it develops a new energetic–acoustic taxonomy grounded in the understanding of instruments as documentary and epistemic objects. The result is a modular, excitation-based system capable of describing the full range of sound-producing objects and their structural possibilities.

The current stage of the project includes:

  • A critical reassessment of the Hornbostel–Sachs system, with special attention to its morphological bias, its difficulties with hybrid and electroacoustic instruments, and the limitations of its present semantic structure (including the 2011 MIMO version).
  • A theoretical reframing of instruments as documentary and epistemic objects, establishing the basis for a more relational and context-aware organology.
  • A new energetic–acoustic taxonomy, organized as a modular decimal system grounded in excitation, acoustic behaviour, and material interaction. The model is designed to be interoperable with RDF/OWL ontologies and to align with existing MIMO categories while addressing areas where the current system remains incomplete.
  • A number of theoretical instrument classes that arise from the internal logic of this decimal structure. These classes represent instrument-types that are structurally coherent within the system but are not yet attested in any known cultural tradition: conceptually possible instruments generated by the completeness of the model.

During 2026 this page will progressively host the technical documentation of the system: an overview of the energetic–acoustic levels, detailed classification tables, and examples of their application to specific instruments.

In addition to the classification scheme itself and its related texts presented on this page, the project includes records for specific musical instruments and an organological index.
