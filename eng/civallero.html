I am a musician, librarian, archivist, memory weaver, and craftsman. For almost three decades, I've worked with words, languages, sounds, silences, bodies, landscapes, and objects — especially those that resonate. In Instrumentarium, all those paths converge.

This digital project is not a collection of instruments or a simple virtual archive: it is a space for action-research, for critical thought and reflection. Here, I experiment with ways of documenting, classifying, and narrating sound. I bring together aerophones and chordophones, taxonomic schemes and podcasts, scores and canes — all driven by the same impulse: to understand how sounds carry memory, how objects know, and how we might listen to and interpret them critically.

I work from the edges. From critical librarianship, insurgent epistemologies, rogue archival practice, and an unruly vision of organology and ethnomusicology. I build taxonomies that don't quite fit, play instruments that are disappearing, create others that never existed, imagine sonic landscapes and auditory cultures, and insist that a drum or a flute can be a document too. And I do this from Latin America, in a workshop full of seeds, scrap, vegetable fibers, plastic, and strings — writing with clay and linked data.

Instrumentarium is not an academic project, though it speaks with the academic world. It is not a catalog, though it classifies. It is a proposal for re-reading: musical instruments, sonic documentation, and the knowledge technologies that are usually left out of view.

This site —like me— does not fully belong to any one discipline. And therein lies its strength.

If you'd like to explore other facets of my work —in libraries, archives, memory, biodiversity, and language— you can visit my main site: edgardocivallero.com.
