The many erquenchos

Sketch 011

Home > Blog A Musician's Log > Sketch 011

 

The erquencho or erque is an aerophone of traditional construction and use in northwestern Argentina and southern Bolivia. It is an idioglottal clarinet: the only single reed instrument present in traditional Andean organology. Basically, it is a reed pipe called boquilla or pajuela, between 8 and 15 cm in length and up to 1 cm in diameter, closed on one side and open on the other, and equipped with a tongue at the proximal end and a huge pavilion at the distal.

There are regional variants of erquencho. In Argentina it is used throughout the province of Jujuy and in the pre-puna area of the province of Salta, in the northwest of the country. In the highlands of Jujuy, it is usually built with goat's horn (due to the scarcity of cattle) and in the east of the same province, with copper or bronze sheet. In Salta there are erquenchos that combine antler and metal and reach considerable sizes. In some modern versions, Argentine builders do not cut the reed from the mouthpiece, but instead make a rectangular hole in it and insert a sheet of X-ray plate or other similar material there. This, by the way, makes the clarinet heteroglottal.

In Bolivia, where the instrument is called erque (probably the original designation) or, according to some authors, huacachupa (from Quechua wakachupa, "cow's tail"), it is found mainly in the department of Tarija, in the south of the country. Its presence has also been detected in the neighboring department of Potosí, in regions such as Calcha, and among the Jalq'a people of the department of Sucre. There, it is very common for the reed to open directly by breaking the knot of the cane at the proximal end, and for the bells to be large, highly ornamented bovine horns.
